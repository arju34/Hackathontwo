Living 🏘️

As you enter college, you must be thinking of where to live when you attend college. Or what would be the best option because finding a living space can be tough whether you want to live on campus, off campus. Living with your parents is also another option if the college you attend is close to home. At home, you wouldn't need to worry about rent or utility bills; and there's less distraction at home than in dorms. You wouldn't need to worry about what to eat or endless grocery runs, some colleges, however, do not have kitchens in their dorms so you'd have to get your meals from the dining hall. However, there are still some negatives with living at home. Here I'll talk about the pros and cons of living on campus, off campus, and with your parents.

Location 🌍

Before considering where you want to live, it is important to consider what type of location you want to live in especially if you are moving to a different state or going to live on your own. Here are some questions to consider:

  • What type of climate do you prefer?
  • Do you like the city life or do you want to have more open space? (urban, suburban, or rural)
  • How far away is it from home? Do you want it to be far or close by?
  • What is the distance from the nearest stores, groceries, and other public locations?
  • How will you move throughout campus? What transportation is available?
These are just some of the questions you should consider for every college you are searching up to have a better sense of what money, time, and resources should be allocated to every living place.

Living On Campus 🏫

Pros: If you live on campus, you can engage in college activites, meet new people/friends, and focus on your school work. A study showed that students who live on campus have a higher graduation rate than those who live off campus. Going to classes would be easier living on campus. If something were to break, you can contact the college to get it fixed. If you don't enjoy cooking, you can also just get food which cuts down on the time and energy of preparing food.
Cons: There are distractions and limited privacy, even if you aquire your own room you would still have to share restrooms and other communal places with other people. Like I stated above, some colleges do not have kitchen so you'd have to run to dining hall for meals and the food there can sometimes be repetitive and sometimes be bland, so you might need to order your meals. You would have to take care of yourself completely and do all the chores required to keep your area clean.


Living Off Campus 🏙️

Pros: You'll probably have your space and privacy, no more sharing restrooms (or at least not shared by as many), and more space and freedom. Private apartments are usually quieter and have fewer distractions, and therefore, are better for studying. You can make your own meals as you will have your own kitchen. Having a rental history will make it easier to get a place after you graduate, plus you'll have the "real world" experience while living in an apartment. It might be better to live an apartment as sometimes it is cheaper in rent than dorming.
Cons: It can become expensive as you've got your months rent, utilites, furniture, groceries, transportation, and more to pay for. For Internet and cable TV connection or literally anything were to brake, you're on your own and you would have to fix it (or pay someone else to fix it). You'll have chores as renters usually spend more time grocery-shopping, preparing meals, than those who live on campus. There are more things you will have to take care of as now the maintenance and other responsibilities are on you, the tenant.


Living With Parents/Family 👨‍👩‍👧‍👦

Pros: When living with your parents or other family members, you won't need to worry about distractions or any bills so you can save a lot of money. Many college grads are choosing to move home to save money especially with this generation. You won't have as much responsibility and you won't get homesick or be overwhelmed by the transition as the area and climate are familiar. You can make a schedule that fits best with you and even get direct help from other family members who have already gone through the college experience.
Cons: The biggest negative being that you will not be exposed to living on your own which will make the transition harder from the getting your own career after college a little harder as you were not exposed to a lot of the things that need to happen when being alone. You also lose out on the college experience of living in a dorm and being part of the activities that are hosted for students living on campus. Sometimes parents and family members may get too involved or invested into your college career which may put more pressure or stress on your learning abilities. Family matters and othe situations will affect you more as you are directly at home and being at home may be more distracting as relatives can barge in making it easier to lose focus.

The Costs of Living 🏷️

If you decide to live on your own, you will have to plan out how much money you are going to be allocating for paying off other expenses of living. This may include buying your own food (or paying for meal plans), paying for laundry, paying for bills, paying for mailing and delivery, and the many other expenses. No matter whether you live at home or alone, you will still have to pay for tuition, fees, textbooks, online services, and other necessary needs for college so it is important to plan for these costs regardless. Even though you may not exactly know how much everything will cost now, it is best to overestimate and keep money saved up that will be used for certain things. Even if you do not have this money right now, you can work for that amount later or use loans to pay for it so it's best to plan accordingly.
